Summary of Results¶

The analysis of skewness and kurtosis for the numeric features in the dataset reveals that most of the features deviate from a perfectly symmetrical normal distribution.

Skewness: The skewness values indicate that several numeric features exhibit positive skewness. This means that their distributions have a longer tail on the right side. The degree of positive skewness varies among the features, with some having a slight skew and others showing a more pronounced asymmetry.

Kurtosis: The kurtosis values suggest that the numeric features have varying degrees of peakedness and tailedness compared to a normal distribution. Some features may have lighter or heavier tails than a normal distribution.

In conclusion, the numeric features in this dataset are generally not normally distributed, exhibiting varying levels of skewness and kurtosis. This information is important for selecting appropriate statistical methods or transformations if normality is an assumption for further analysis.

Dendrogram¶

A dendrogram visualizes the hierarchical clustering of variables based on their nullity correlation (how often they are missing together).

The lines in the dendrogram represent the distance between clusters of variables based on their missingness. The closer the lines are joined at the bottom, the more correlated the missingness is between those variables.

Similar to the heatmap, since this dataset has no missing values, there is no missingness to cluster.

Estimate Skewness and Kurtosis¶

Skewness measures the asymmetry of the distribution of data around its mean.

Kurtosis measures the "tailedness" of the distribution, indicating how heavily the tails differ from the tails of a normal distribution.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import seaborn as sns
import scipy.stats as st

y = ds['mpg']

# Plot with Johnson SU distribution
plt.figure(1)
plt.title('Johnson SU')
sns.histplot(y, kde=False, stat='density') # Use histplot and stat='density'
xmin, xmax = plt.xlim()
x = np.linspace(xmin, xmax, 100)
p = st.johnsonsu.pdf(x, *st.johnsonsu.fit(y))
plt.plot(x, p, 'k', linewidth=2)


# Plot with Normal distribution
plt.figure(2)
plt.title('Normal')
sns.histplot(y, kde=False, stat='density') # Use histplot and stat='density'
xmin, xmax = plt.xlim()
x = np.linspace(xmin, xmax, 100)
p = st.norm.pdf(x, *st.norm.fit(y))
plt.plot(x, p, 'k', linewidth=2)


# Plot with Log Normal distribution
plt.figure(3)
plt.title('Log Normal')
sns.histplot(y, kde=False, stat='density') # Use histplot and stat='density'
xmin, xmax = plt.xlim()
x = np.linspace(xmin, xmax, 100)
p = st.lognorm.pdf(x, *st.lognorm.fit(y))
plt.plot(x, p, 'k', linewidth=2)

plt.show()

The Johnson SU distribution is a very flexible four-parameter distribution that can fit a wide variety of data shapes, including skewed and heavy-tailed distributions. The Log Normal distribution is a two-parameter distribution that is also used for positively skewed data.

It's possible that for the specific characteristics of the 'mpg' data, both the Log Normal and Johnson SU distributions provide a reasonably good and visually similar fit. While they are different theoretical distributions, their shapes can overlap depending on the parameters fitted to the data.
